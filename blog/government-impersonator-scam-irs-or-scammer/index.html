Government Impersonator Scam — Is It the IRS or a Scammer?

Updated April 2026 - 6 min read

You received a call, email, or letter claiming to be from the IRS, Social Security Administration, Medicare, or another government agency. They said you owe money, face arrest, or your benefits are being suspended. It felt urgent and official.

Here is how to know immediately whether it was real — and what to do if you already paid.

The IRS will NEVER call demanding immediate payment. The IRS will NEVER ask for gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency. The IRS will NEVER threaten you with arrest. These are 100 percent scam tactics.

How Real Government Agencies Actually Contact You

  • The IRS contacts you first by mail — always. Never by phone as a first contact.
  • Social Security will never suspend your number over the phone
  • Real government agents never demand immediate payment
  • Real agencies never ask for payment in gift cards, Zelle, Venmo, wire transfer, or crypto
  • Real agencies give you the right to dispute any claim in writing

The Red Flags of Government Impersonator Scams

  • Urgency and threats — arrest, deportation, license suspension
  • Secrecy — told not to tell your family or bank
  • Unusual payment methods — gift cards, wire, crypto
  • Spoofed caller ID showing official government numbers
  • Robo-calls with official-sounding automated messages
  • Pressure to stay on the phone and act immediately

What To Do If You Already Paid

  1. If you paid by gift card, call the gift card company immediately and report fraud
  2. If you wired money, call your bank immediately and request a wire recall
  3. If you paid by Zelle or Venmo, contact their fraud departments
  4. File a report with the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov
  5. Report to the Treasury Inspector General at tigta.gov if IRS was impersonated
  6. Report to the Social Security OIG at oig.ssa.gov if SSA was impersonated
  7. File a local police report

How To Verify If a Government Contact Is Real

  • Hang up and call the official number yourself — find it on the official .gov website directly, not from the caller
  • For IRS: call 1-800-829-1040
  • For Social Security: call 1-800-772-1213
  • For Medicare: call 1-800-633-4227
  • Never call back numbers provided in suspicious emails, texts, or calls

Important: You will not be arrested for hanging up

Scammers rely on fear to stop you from thinking clearly. Real government agents will never threaten immediate arrest over the phone. Hanging up on a scammer is always the right move.
